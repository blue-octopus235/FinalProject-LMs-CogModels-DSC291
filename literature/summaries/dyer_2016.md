**PDF:** https://aclanthology.org/N16-1024.pdf

## What the paper does

Introduces **Recurrent Neural Network Grammars (RNNGs)**, a generative model over both word sequences and their hierarchical phrase-structure parse trees. The model operates via a stack-based transition system with three actions:

- **SHIFT**: consume the next word from the buffer and push it onto the stack
- **NT(X)**: open a new non-terminal node of type X on the stack
- **REDUCE**: pop the top stack elements and compose them into a completed constituent

At each step an LSTM encodes the current stack, input buffer, and action history to predict the next action or word. Because the model explicitly constructs a parse tree during generation, it has a hard inductive bias toward hierarchical phrase structure — unlike a plain LSTM that processes tokens left-to-right with no structural bottleneck.

The paper reports strong language modeling perplexity on PTB, competitive with state-of-the-art LSTMs at the time, suggesting the structural inductive bias does not hurt fluency.

## Relevance to our project

- **Primary syntactic-bias architecture**: The RNNG is our main alternative to the LSTM. Our hypothesis is that RNNG's explicit hierarchical inductive bias makes it *more robust to noisy agreement training data* than the sequential LSTM.
- **Implementation reference**: We adapt or re-use an existing RNNG implementation (see `notebooks/rnng_spike.ipynb`). This paper defines the model's action vocabulary, loss function, and inference procedure.
- **Supervision requirement**: RNNG requires **bracketed parse trees** for training, which means we need a parsed version of the Linzen corpus. This is the biggest compute/engineering risk for the project (see README).
- **RNNG vs. LSTM as core comparison**: Wilcox et al. (2020) and Sartran et al. (2022) both build on this architecture to show improved syntactic generalization relative to LSTMs — our study adds the noise dimension to that comparison.

## Key terms

- **Stack LSTM**: the specific LSTM variant used to encode the parse stack
- **Transition system**: the SHIFT/NT/REDUCE action set that generates trees
- **Discriminative vs. generative RNNG**: the paper introduces both; the generative version is more commonly used for language modeling
